# Company Formation and Company Secretarial

Setting up a limited company is, on the face of it, quick and cheap — it can be done online in an afternoon. What catches people out is everything that comes afterwards. A company is a legal entity in its own right, and that status brings a continuing set of obligations to Companies House that don't go away just because the business is small, dormant, or having a quiet year. The rules have also tightened considerably under recent reforms, with identity checks now a legal requirement for the people who run companies.

This guide walks through both halves of the picture: forming a company properly in the first place, and the ongoing "company secretarial" work needed to keep it compliant. Whether you're a dentist incorporating a practice, a contractor setting up a personal company, or an established business adding a new entity, the essentials below will help you start on the right footing and stay there.

## What Forming a Company Involves

A UK limited company is registered ("incorporated") at Companies House. To set one up you need to decide and provide:

- **A company name** that isn't already taken and doesn't breach the naming rules

- **A registered office address** — a valid UK address where official post is received; since March 2024 a PO box alone is no longer acceptable

- **At least one director**, who is legally responsible for running the company and meeting its obligations

- **At least one shareholder** (who can be the same person as the director)

- **Details of people with significant control** — broadly, anyone owning or controlling more than 25% of the shares or voting rights

- **A SIC code**, which describes the company's main business activity

- **Memorandum and articles of association** — the constitutional documents; most companies use the standard model articles

Incorporating online through Companies House currently costs **£100** (raised from £50 on 1 February 2026), with a same-day digital service at **£156**. Many people instead use a formation agent or accountant, who handles the submission and often bundles in the registers and documents you'll need from day one.

## Identity Verification — the Big Recent Change

This is the development anyone forming or running a company now has to reckon with. Under the Economic Crime and Corporate Transparency Act (ECCTA), **identity verification became a legal requirement from 18 November 2025**. For decades you could become a UK director by supplying little more than a name and address; that era is over, and the change is aimed squarely at fraud and misuse of the register.

The essentials:

- Every **director** and every **person with significant control** must verify their identity with Companies House

- For **new incorporations and new appointments** from 18 November 2025, verification must happen _before_ the company is formed or the appointment is registered

- **Existing** directors and PSCs have a transition period ending **18 November 2026**, but in practice the trigger is usually your company's next confirmation statement — often well before that backstop

- Verification is a **one-time** process that gives you a unique personal code, reused across any companies you're involved with

Verification can be done free through GOV.UK One Login (using a government-issued photo ID and a smartphone for a liveness check) or via an Authorised Corporate Service Provider — a regulated agent such as an accountant or solicitor. Missing the deadline isn't a minor matter: Companies House can impose financial penalties, pursue criminal proceedings for persistent non-compliance, and in the most serious cases strike the company off.

## What "Company Secretarial" Actually Means

Company secretarial work is the ongoing administration that keeps a company legally compliant — the statutory housekeeping, separate from bookkeeping or tax. Private companies are no longer required to appoint a formal company secretary, so in most small companies **the directors absorb these duties themselves**, whether they realise it or not. The main responsibilities include:

- Filing the **annual confirmation statement** (more below)

- Maintaining the company's **statutory registers**, including the register of members (shareholders)

- Reporting **changes** promptly — new or resigning directors, a change of registered office, share allotments, or changes in people with significant control

- Keeping the **PSC information** accurate and up to date

- Ensuring **annual accounts** are filed on time (a separate obligation, covered in our accounts guidance)

It sounds modest, and for a stable single-director company it often is. The risk is that it's easy to forget — there's no manager chasing you — and the penalties for missed or late filings are automatic.

## The Confirmation Statement

The confirmation statement (form CS01) is the filing that catches the most people out, because it's separate from the accounts and is required **even if nothing has changed** and even if the company is dormant. Its purpose is simply to confirm that the information Companies House holds — directors, registered office, shareholders, SIC codes, PSCs — is correct.

- It must be filed **at least once every 12 months**

- The review period runs from incorporation, or from your last statement, and you have **14 days** after it ends to file

- Online filing costs **£50** (raised from £34 on 1 February 2026); the paper CS01 costs £110

- One fee covers the whole 12-month period, however many times you update details within it

Crucially, identity verification now intersects with this filing. Existing directors must have verified their identity and supplied their personal code before the company files its next confirmation statement — which is why, for most companies, the confirmation statement date is the real verification deadline rather than the November 2026 backstop. Failing to file at all is a criminal offence and can lead to the company being struck off the register.

## Authorised Corporate Service Providers

One further change is worth knowing if you rely on a professional to handle your filings. Accountants, solicitors, and formation agents who file at Companies House on a company's behalf are being brought into the regime as **Authorised Corporate Service Providers (ACSPs)**, a registered and supervised status. In time, only ACSP-registered agents will be able to file for you, and verification requirements are expected to extend to those presenting filings later in the rollout. In practice this means the firm handling your company secretarial work needs to be properly registered — worth confirming if you outsource it.

## Why It's Worth Getting Right

Company formation and secretarial work rarely feel urgent, which is exactly why they slip. Getting them right brings concrete benefits:

- **Avoiding penalties** — missed confirmation statements, late filings, and unverified directors all carry consequences that are entirely avoidable

- **Protecting the company's standing** — a company in good standing on the register is essential for banking, lending, and any due diligence

- **Clean records** — late or incorrect filings are a visible red flag during bank applications, investment, and property transactions

- **Peace of mind** — knowing deadlines are tracked and met removes a quiet, recurring source of risk

## A Few Habits Worth Building

- **Verify your identity early** rather than waiting for the confirmation statement to force it

- **Diarise the confirmation statement date** and the accounts deadline the moment the company is formed

- **Report changes promptly** — a new director or share transfer needs filing within the relevant window, not at year-end

- **Keep the registers current**, particularly the register of members and PSC details

- **Check your agent is an ACSP** if you outsource filings, so they can continue to act for you

A company is straightforward to start and not difficult to maintain — but it does demand a little ongoing attention, and the recent reforms have raised the stakes for getting it wrong. The fees and rules above reflect the position at the time of writing, including the changes that took effect in late 2025 and February 2026, though Companies House requirements continue to evolve. If you'd rather not track it all yourself, a company secretarial service or accountant can carry the burden — which, given how easily a missed deadline can escalate, is often a sensible investment.
