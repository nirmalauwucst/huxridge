# Making Tax Digital: A Practical Guide

Making Tax Digital — MTD — is the biggest change to how UK taxpayers interact with HMRC in a generation. The idea behind it is straightforward: move tax record-keeping and reporting out of shoeboxes and spreadsheets and into software that talks directly to HMRC. The execution, spread over several years and several taxes, is where it gets more complicated, and where many sole traders, landlords, and company directors are understandably unsure what actually applies to them and when.

This guide cuts through it. It explains what MTD is, where it already applies, the income tax rollout that's arriving in stages from April 2026, what quarterly reporting really means in practice, and the steps worth taking now. Whether you run a dental practice, contract through a company, or hold rental property alongside your main work, the points below will tell you where you stand.

## What Making Tax Digital Actually Is

At its core, MTD has three requirements for those it applies to:

- **Keep digital records** — your income and expenses must be recorded in software, not on paper or in a basic spreadsheet kept to one side

- **Use compatible software** — records are kept and submissions made through HMRC-recognised software, with a digital link all the way through to HMRC

- **Submit more often** — for income tax, that means quarterly updates through the year rather than a single annual return

The aim, in HMRC's words, is a more accurate and efficient tax system with fewer errors. For the taxpayer, the honest picture is mixed: more frequent admin but offset by better visibility of your position through the year and less of the January scramble that the old once-a-year system encouraged.

## MTD for VAT: Already Here

If you're VAT-registered, you're already living with MTD. Since April 2022, **every VAT-registered business** — regardless of turnover — has had to keep digital records and file VAT returns through MTD-compatible software. The old HMRC online portal for typing in VAT figures is closed to these businesses.

A reassuring point for anyone anxious about the income tax changes: MTD for VAT didn't change _when_ you file or pay. VAT return and payment deadlines remain one calendar month and seven days after the end of your VAT period — only the method of filing changed, from portal to software. The income tax rollout follows the same philosophy: changing the mechanism rather than inventing new payment dates.

## MTD for Income Tax: The Big One, Arriving in Stages

This is the change most people are now preparing for. MTD for Income Tax (sometimes written MTD ITSA) applies to **sole traders and landlords**, and it's being phased in by income level. The dates and thresholds, confirmed by the government, are:

- From **April 2026** — those with qualifying income over **£50,000** (based on the 2024/25 tax return)

- From **April 2027** — those with qualifying income over **£30,000** (based on the 2025/26 tax return)

- From **April 2028** — those with qualifying income over **£20,000** (based on the 2026/27 tax return)

Partnerships will be brought in later, on a timeline HMRC has yet to confirm, and most trusts aren't expected to be included. HMRC estimates that more than 860,000 sole traders and landlords come into scope in that very first April 2026 wave alone.

## "Qualifying Income" — the Detail That Catches People

The threshold isn't based on profit, and it isn't based on any single income source. **Qualifying income is your combined gross income from self-employment and property**, added together, before any expenses. Two things follow from that, and both trip people up:

- It's **gross**, not net — a sole trader turning over £55,000 is in scope even if their profit after costs is far lower

- It's **combined** — someone with £35,000 of self-employed income and £25,000 of rental income has £60,000 of qualifying income and is in the April 2026 wave, even though neither source alone crosses £50,000

It's also assessed _per individual_. For jointly held property, it's your share of the gross rents that counts. And one quirk worth flagging: if a return showing qualifying income above the threshold is filed late, you can still be treated as mandated from the original start date — so the obligation can effectively be backdated.

## How Quarterly Reporting Works

This is the part that feels daunting and turns out to be more manageable than it sounds. Under MTD for Income Tax, you submit **four quarterly updates** through the year, plus a **Final Declaration** after the year ends. The standard quarterly deadlines are fixed:

- **Quarter 1** (6 April – 5 July) — due **7 August**

- **Quarter 2** (6 July – 5 October) — due **7 November**

- **Quarter 3** (6 October – 5 January) — due **7 February**

- **Quarter 4** (6 January – 5 April) — due **7 May**

Each update is just a running summary of income and expenses for the period — not a tax calculation, and not final. Mistakes can be corrected later without penalty, so the priority is filing on time with your best figures rather than agonising over precision. You can opt to use calendar quarters (ending March, June, September, December) instead, which is worth doing if you're VAT-registered so the periods line up; you just need to choose that option in your software before your first update.

After the fourth update, the **Final Declaration** replaces the old Self-Assessment return. It's where you confirm your full-year figures, claim allowances and reliefs, bring in any other income, and finalise what you owe. It's due by **31 January** following the end of the tax year — the same familiar date as the current system. For the first 2026/27 cohort, that means a Final Declaration by 31 January 2028.

## Reporting Changes — Paying Doesn't

This is the single most important reassurance, because it's where the anxiety usually sits. Quarterly updates do **not** mean quarterly tax bills. What changes is how often you _report_, not how often you _pay_. Your tax is still calculated at the year end and paid under the existing January timetable, with payments on account where they already apply. The quarterly figures give HMRC — and you — a running estimate, but they don't trigger a payment.

## Penalties and the First-Year Soft Landing

MTD for Income Tax uses a **points-based penalty system** for late updates, working much like points on a driving licence. You collect one point per missed quarterly deadline, and a **£200 penalty** applies once you reach four points. Points reset after a sustained period of compliance.

Crucially, HMRC has confirmed a **soft landing for 2026/27**: no penalty points for late quarterly updates during that first year for the initial cohort. That said, the soft landing does _not_ cover the Final Declaration, which remains under the normal late-filing rules, and it offers no protection against late-payment penalties or interest, which run on a separate regime entirely. The grace applies to the new quarterly habit, not to paying your tax late.

## Are You Exempt?

Some people fall outside MTD. Exemptions exist, though several require an application to HMRC rather than being automatic. They include:

- Those who are **digitally excluded** — for example, where age, disability, or genuine lack of internet access makes digital record-keeping impractical

- Certain groups such as some ministers of religion, recipients of Blind Person's Allowance, and foreign entertainers

- Those with qualifying income below the relevant threshold — who continue to report through Self-Assessment as now

If you're exempt you still have to report your income the traditional way; exemption is from the digital mechanism, not from tax itself.

## What to Do Now

Preparation is genuinely the difference between a smooth transition and a stressful one. If you think you're in scope:

- **Check your qualifying income** — take the relevant year's tax return and add your self-employment turnover to your gross property income

- **Choose MTD-compatible software** early, and make sure it's recognised for income tax — not just for VAT

- **Start keeping digital records** from day one of the relevant tax year rather than waiting for the first deadline

- **Sign up with HMRC** — you aren't enrolled automatically; it's an active sign-up, though you can also volunteer early to test the system

- **Diarise the quarterly dates** — 7 August, 7 November, 7 February, and 7 May for a standard year

- **Do a dry run** if your software allows, so the mechanics and bank feeds are tested before they count

MTD is a meaningful change in habit, but it isn't a change in how much tax you owe or when you pay it — and the businesses that adapt early tend to find the quarterly rhythm becomes routine quickly. The dates and thresholds above reflect the confirmed position at the time of writing, though details can shift with government announcements. If you're unsure whether you're in scope or which software fits your situation, a short conversation with an accountant is well worth it — getting the setup right once saves a great deal of correction later.
