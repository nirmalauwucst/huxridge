# Personal Tax: What You Actually Need to Know

Most people first really pay attention to their personal tax position when something goes slightly wrong. A tax code looks off. A Self Assessment deadline appears out of nowhere. A dividend payment turns out to be taxable when you assumed it wasn't. By then you're reacting rather than planning, and reacting is almost always the more expensive option.

Whether you're a nurse picking up bank shifts, a locum working across several practices, a consultant with private income on the side, or a company director paying yourself through a mix of salary and dividends, the underlying rules are the same. What changes is how those rules land on your particular set of circumstances. This guide walks through the parts that matter for the 2025/26 tax year, with the actual figures rather than vague reassurances.

## The Personal Allowance, and Why It Quietly Shrinks for Higher Earners

The Personal Allowance is the slice of income you can earn before income tax applies. For 2025/26 it sits at **£12,570**, exactly where it has been since April 2022, and current legislation keeps it frozen until April 2028. That freeze matters more than it sounds. As wages rise with inflation, a static allowance pulls more people into tax and nudges others into higher bands without any rate ever changing on paper. It's sometimes called fiscal drag, and it's doing quite a lot of heavy lifting in the current system.

The standard tax code attached to this allowance is **1257L**, which you'll see on most payslips.

There's a catch that surprises a lot of people the first time their income climbs:

- Once your income passes **£100,000**, your Personal Allowance reduces by £1 for every £2 you earn above that line.

- By the time you reach **£125,140**, the allowance has vanished entirely.

The practical effect is a band of income between £100,000 and £125,140 where you're effectively taxed at around 60%, because you're losing tax-free allowance at the same time as paying tax on the income itself. If you're anywhere near that zone — and plenty of senior clinicians and directors are — pension contributions or charitable giving can pull your adjusted net income back down and restore some or all of the allowance. This is one of the few areas where a bit of forward planning genuinely changes the number.

## Income Tax Bands for 2025/26

For England, Wales, and Northern Ireland, the bands work like this once your Personal Allowance is used up:

- **Basic rate (20%)** — on taxable income up to £37,700, which means income up to £50,270 in total

- **Higher rate (40%)** — on income from £50,271 to £125,140

- **Additional rate (45%)** — on income above £125,140

All three rates and thresholds are unchanged from the previous year.

Scotland runs its own system, and it's genuinely different rather than a minor variation. Scottish taxpayers face six bands ranging from a 19% starter rate up to a 48% top rate, with the higher-rate threshold kicking in earlier than it does elsewhere in the UK. If you live in Scotland, the rest-of-UK figures won't describe your bill accurately, so it's worth checking the Scottish bands specifically.

## National Insurance: The Tax That Isn't Called a Tax

National Insurance comes out of earned income alongside income tax, and for employees, the main rate is now **8%** for 2025/26. That rate was cut twice in fairly quick succession — from 12% to 10%, then 10% to 8% — so if you remember paying more, you're not misremembering.

For employees, the rough shape is:

- **Nothing** on weekly earnings below the primary threshold of £242

- **8%** on earnings between £242 and £967 a week

- **2%** on anything above £967 a week

If you're self-employed, a sole trader, or a partner — which covers a good number of locums and independent practitioners — you'll pay Class 4 NICs through Self-Assessment instead, calculated on your profits rather than deducted as you go.

## Self-Employed and Locum Income

If you work for yourself or take on locum and agency work outside of PAYE, the responsibility for declaring income shifts onto you. There's no employer quietly handling the deductions in the background.

A few things worth holding in mind:

- You'll generally need to **register for Self-Assessment** and file a return each year, with the online deadline of **31 January** following the end of the tax year. Tax owed is due on the same date.

- Many people also make **payments on account** — advance instalments towards the next year's bill — which can come as an unpleasant surprise in that first January if nobody warns you.

- The **trading allowance** lets you earn up to £1,000 of self-employed or casual income before you need to report it at all. Genuinely useful for a small side income; irrelevant once you're earning properly from it.

- Keep records as you go rather than reconstructing them in a panic each January. Expenses you can't evidence are expenses you can't claim.

The truth is that the self-employed route gives you very little control over when tax falls due or at what rate. Profit is profit, whether you reinvest it or spend it, and it's taxed regardless.

## Company Directors: Salary, Dividends, and the April 2026 Shift

Directors of their own limited companies sit in a different position again, usually paying themselves through a combination of a modest salary and dividends drawn from company profits. It's a structure that has long offered some efficiency, though that advantage has been narrowing.

Dividends are taxed on top of your other income — they sit last in the stacking order, after salary, pension, and rental income — so the rest of your income determines which dividend band they fall into. For 2025/26:

- Every individual gets a **£500 dividend allowance**, taxed at 0%. Worth noting this has shrunk considerably: it was £2,000 not long ago, then £1,000, now £500.

- Above the allowance, dividends are taxed at **8.75% (basic rate)**, **33.75% (higher rate)**, and **39.35% (additional rate)** for 2025/26.

Here's the part directors should pay close attention to. **From 6 April 2026, the basic and higher dividend rates rise** — the basic rate moves to 10.75% and the higher rate to 35.75%, while the additional rate stays at 39.35%. If you control the timing of your own dividends, the gap between the current year and the next one is worth modelling properly rather than assuming last year's approach still holds.

A couple of further points for directors:

- Dividend income above £10,000 needs to be reported through **Self-Assessment**.

- Dividends can only be paid from genuine retained profit after corporation tax. Drawing money the company hasn't actually earned and labelling it a dividend is the kind of thing that causes real problems if HMRC reclassifies it as salary or a loan.

- Because dividend rates are climbing, it's no longer safe to assume that incorporation automatically beats operating as a sole trader. At some profit levels, it still does; at others, the margin has closed. Run both before deciding.

## Allowances People Routinely Forget

These are the ones that get left on the table year after year:

- **Marriage Allowance** — if one partner earns below £12,570 and the other is a basic-rate taxpayer, the lower earner can transfer £1,260 of unused allowance across. That's worth up to £252 a year, and you can backdate a claim up to four years. HMRC's own estimates suggest a large number of eligible couples have simply never claimed it.

- **Personal Savings Allowance** — basic-rate taxpayers can receive £1,000 of savings interest tax-free; higher-rate taxpayers get £500; additional-rate taxpayers get nothing. With interest rates where they've been, more people are bumping into this than they used to.

- **ISAs** — income and gains inside an ISA fall outside tax entirely. Dividends paid on shares held in an ISA aren't taxable and don't need declaring, which makes them a sensible home for investment income if you're regularly exceeding the dividend allowance.

- **High Income Child Benefit Charge** — if you or your partner has income above £60,000 and you receive Child Benefit, a charge claws some of it back: 1% of the benefit for every £200 of income over £60,000, with the full amount recovered by £80,000. Easy to overlook, awkward to unwind after the fact.

## A Few Habits That Save Money and Stress

None of this is complicated in isolation. The difficulty is that the pieces interact, and the cost of getting them wrong tends to show up months later with interest attached. A handful of habits go a long way:

- **Check your tax code** at the start of each tax year and whenever your income changes. PAYE adjusts cumulatively, so an error left uncorrected compounds across the year.

- **Don't ignore a Self-Assessment obligation** in the hope it goes away. Late filing and late payment penalties accrue, and they're entirely avoidable.

- **Think about timing** if you have any control over it — dividends, bonuses, pension contributions, and the order in which income falls can all shift your eventual bill.

- **Keep your records current**, not retrospective. The version of you in January will be grateful.

Personal tax rewards attention and punishes neglect, which is a slightly unforgiving design but a consistent one. The figures above are accurate for the 2025/26 tax year, though thresholds and rates do move — and where your circumstances are anything other than straightforward, a conversation with an accountant usually pays for itself several times over.
