# VAT: A Practical Guide for UK Businesses

VAT — Value Added Tax — is the tax most businesses encounter the moment they start to grow. It's charged on most goods and services sold in the UK, and once a business crosses a certain size it has no choice but to register, charge it, and account for it to HMRC. For many owners it's the most administratively demanding tax they deal with, partly because it's ongoing rather than annual, and partly because the rules around rates, schemes, and registration have a few traps that catch the unwary.

This guide explains how VAT works in plain terms: when you have to register, the rates that apply, the schemes that can make life easier, the deadlines, and the penalties for getting it wrong. Whether you run a dental practice, a healthcare business, a professional firm, or a growing company of any kind, the essentials below will help you understand where you stand.

## What VAT Actually Is

VAT is a consumption tax. A VAT-registered business charges VAT on what it sells ("output VAT"), reclaims the VAT it pays on its own purchases ("input VAT"), and pays HMRC the difference. In effect, the business acts as an unpaid tax collector — the cost ultimately falls on the final consumer, while the business passes the net amount up the chain to HMRC.

That mechanism is why VAT registration is double-edged. You take on the job of charging and reporting it, but you also gain the ability to recover VAT on your costs, which, for some businesses, more than justifies registering even before it's compulsory.

## When You Must Register

Registration becomes compulsory once your **taxable turnover** crosses the threshold, which for 2025/26 is **£90,000**. This figure has been frozen at that level since it rose from £85,000 in April 2024, and the deregistration threshold sits just below it at **£88,000**. The points that trip people up:

- It's a **rolling 12-month test**, not a financial or calendar year — you could breach it in any month, so it needs watching continuously

- You must also register if you **expect to exceed £90,000 in the next 30 days alone**

- Once over the threshold, you have **30 days** from the end of the month in which you exceeded it to register

- The threshold is based on **taxable turnover** — standard, reduced, and zero-rated sales — not profit, and it isn't pro-rated for part-year businesses

Missed the deadline because turnover genuinely tipped over, and you'll owe the VAT from the date you should have registered, potentially with a penalty on top. It's one of the more common and avoidable mistakes, which is why tracking turnover on a rolling basis matters.

You can also register **voluntarily** below the threshold. This makes sense where most of your customers are themselves VAT-registered (so the VAT you charge them costs them nothing) and you have meaningful input VAT to reclaim on your own costs. For a business selling mainly to the public, though, voluntary registration simply makes you 20% more expensive, so it's a decision worth weighing carefully.

## The VAT Rates

The UK has three VAT rates, unchanged for 2025/26, plus categories that sit outside the system entirely:

- **Standard rate — 20%**: the default for most goods and services, from professional fees and electronics to restaurant meals and software

- **Reduced rate — 5%**: a defined list including domestic gas and electricity, children's car seats, mobility aids, and certain energy-saving installations

- **Zero rate — 0%**: items such as most food, books, and children's clothing. These are still taxable supplies, which matters — a zero-rated business stays in the VAT system and can reclaim input VAT

Separately, some supplies are **exempt** — including many financial services, insurance, education from eligible bodies, and health services from registered practitioners. This is the crucial distinction for healthcare businesses: exempt is not the same as zero-rated. A business making only exempt supplies cannot register for VAT and cannot reclaim input VAT on its costs. If you make both taxable and exempt supplies you're "partly exempt," which brings its own rules about how much input VAT you can recover — an area where professional advice usually pays for itself.

## VAT Schemes That Can Help

Standard VAT accounting works on an invoice basis: you account for VAT when an invoice is issued or received, regardless of whether the money has actually moved. For many smaller businesses, one of HMRC's optional schemes is easier or kinder to cash flow.

The **Flat Rate Scheme** is built for simplicity:

- Available to businesses expecting taxable turnover of **£150,000** or less (excluding VAT)

- You pay HMRC a **fixed percentage of your gross turnover** — set by your sector — rather than working out output minus input VAT on every transaction

- You generally can't reclaim input VAT (except on capital assets over £2,000), so it suits service businesses with low costs

- You must leave once turnover exceeds **£230,000**

The **Cash Accounting Scheme** helps cash flow directly:

- Open to businesses with taxable turnover of **£1.35 million** or less

- You account for VAT only when money actually changes hands — so you don't pay over VAT on an invoice until the customer has paid you

- Strong protection against bad debts and late payers, though you can only reclaim input VAT once you've paid your own suppliers

- You must leave once turnover exceeds **£1.6 million**

The **Annual Accounting Scheme** reduces paperwork:

- Also for businesses with turnover of **£1.35 million** or less

- You file **one VAT return a year** instead of four, making advance payments through the year (nine monthly or three quarterly instalments) and settling up at the end

- It eases administration but relies on decent forecasting — you can overpay during quieter months and wait for the balance back

These schemes aren't mutually exclusive in every case, and the right choice depends on your turnover, your cost base, and how your customers pay. Some lock you in for a period, so it's worth getting advice before committing — the wrong scheme can quietly cost more than it saves.

## Filing and Deadlines

Most businesses file VAT returns **quarterly**, though monthly filing is available and often suits businesses that are regularly in a refund position, such as exporters. Whatever the frequency, the deadline rule is consistent:

- VAT returns and payment are both due **one calendar month and seven days** after the end of the VAT period

- For example, for a quarter ending **31 March**, the return and payment are due by **7 May**

All VAT filing is now done through **Making Tax Digital**. Since April 2022, every VAT-registered business — regardless of turnover — must keep digital records and submit returns through MTD-compatible software. The old HMRC online portal for typing in figures is closed; the process has to be digital from record-keeping through to submission. Importantly, MTD changed the _method_, not the deadlines — the month-and-seven-days rule is unchanged.

## Penalties for Getting It Wrong

HMRC uses a **points-based penalty system** for late VAT returns, much like points on a driving licence. You collect a point for each late return, and a **£200 penalty** applies once you reach the threshold — four points for quarterly filers — with a further £200 for each subsequent late return while you're at the threshold. Points clear after a sustained period of compliance.

Late _payment_ is handled separately: interest runs on overdue VAT from the day it was due, and percentage-based penalties build the longer payment is outstanding. On top of all this, **inaccurate returns** can attract penalties of up to 100% of the VAT involved where errors are careless or deliberate. None of these is usually catastrophic for a single slip, but they add up quickly for a business that repeatedly misses deadlines.

## A Few Habits Worth Building

- **Watch turnover on a rolling basis**, not just at year-end — the registration test can be triggered in any month

- **Set the VAT aside as you collect it** — it was never your money, and a separate account stops the quarterly bill becoming a shock

- **Use a Direct Debit** set up a few working days before the deadline so payment is collected automatically and on time

- **Review your scheme periodically** — the right choice at registration may not be the right one as you grow

- **Get the rate right on every supply**, and take advice on partial exemption if you make a mix of taxable and exempt sales

VAT rewards good record-keeping and steady routine, and punishes the last-minute scramble. The thresholds, rates, and deadlines above reflect the position for the 2025/26 tax year, but VAT rules and scheme limits do change — and given how easily registration timing and partial exemption can go wrong, a conversation with an accountant is usually a sound investment, particularly around the point of registration.

_This article is general guidance for the 2025/26 UK tax year and reflects HMRC's VAT rules and rates at the time of writing. It is not a substitute for professional advice. For help with registration, scheme choice, or partial exemption, speak to a qualified accountant._
