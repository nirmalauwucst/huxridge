# Annual Accounts: A Plain Guide for Company Directors

If you run a limited company, annual accounts are one of those obligations that feel abstract right up until a deadline is bearing down on you. They're not optional, they're not something you can quietly skip in a quiet year, and the penalties for getting them wrong are automatic — there's no warning letter and no sympathetic phone call first. The good news is that the requirements are more navigable than they look, particularly for the small and micro-sized companies that make up the overwhelming majority of the register.

Whether you're a dentist running a practice through a company, a contractor with a single-director setup, a locum who incorporated a few years back, or someone managing a growing healthcare business, the framework below applies to you. Here's what annual accounts actually are, who has to file what, and the dates that matter for 2025/26.

## What Annual Accounts Actually Are

"Annual accounts" — also called statutory accounts — are the formal financial statements your company prepares at the end of each financial year. They're drawn from your bookkeeping records and pulled together into a standard set of documents. Depending on your company's size, a full set can include:

- A **balance sheet**, showing what the company owns and owes on the last day of the financial year

- A **profit and loss account**, showing income, costs, and the resulting profit or loss across the year

- **Notes to the accounts**, explaining the figures in more detail

- A **director's report** (not required for micro-entities)

- An **auditor's report**, but only if the company is large enough to require an audit

Two separate bodies receive your financial information, and this is the single most common source of confusion. **Companies House** receives the statutory accounts, which go on the public record. **HMRC** receives a Company Tax Return (the CT600), along with accounts and tax computations, to work out your Corporation Tax. Same underlying numbers, two different destinations, two different deadlines.

## Company Size: Why It Changes Everything

How much you have to prepare and file depends entirely on your company's size. The thresholds were increased on 6 April 2025 — the first uplift since 2013 — to ease the reporting burden and account for inflation, so older guidance you might find online will quote lower figures.

For accounting periods beginning on or after 6 April 2025, your company is **small** if it meets at least two of these three conditions:

- Turnover of **£15 million** or less

- Balance sheet total of **£7.5 million** or less

- **50 employees** or fewer

Your company is a **micro-entity** — the smallest category, with the lightest requirements — if it meets at least two of these:

- Turnover of **£1 million** or less (up from £632,000)

- Balance sheet total of **£500,000** or less (up from £316,000)

- **10 employees** or fewer

A useful point: if you qualify as a micro-entity, you automatically qualify as small too. To satisfy the size criteria you generally need to meet the conditions in two out of the last three accounting periods, which stops a single unusual year from bouncing you between categories. Anything above the small thresholds is medium-sized or large, and those companies must prepare fuller accounts.

## What Smaller Companies Can Leave Out

The size categories exist precisely so that a one-person company isn't held to the same standard as a national chain. Micro-entities, in particular, get a genuinely stripped-back deal:

- They can file a **simplified balance sheet** with only basic notes

- They don't have to send a profit and loss account or a director's report to Companies House, which keeps sensitive figures off the public record

- They prepare accounts under **FRS 105**, the reporting standard designed for the very smallest companies

Small companies that aren't quite micro-entities use FRS 102 (Section 1A) and have historically been able to file "filleted" accounts — essentially the full accounts minus the director's report and profit and loss. Be aware that this is changing: under the Economic Crime and Corporate Transparency Act, for accounts filed on or after **1 April 2027**, small companies will have to file both their profit and loss account and director's report, and abridged accounts will no longer be an option. It's worth knowing now if you've grown used to keeping your numbers private.

## Do You Need an Audit?

Most small companies don't. If your company qualifies as small under the thresholds above, it can usually claim **audit exemption**, meaning no independent auditor is required — a significant saving in both cost and hassle. That said, an audit can still be unavoidable in certain situations, including where:

- Shareholders holding at least **10%** of the share capital demand one

- Your company is part of a larger group that doesn't qualify as small

- Your articles of association or industry regulator require it

- The company is of a type excluded from exemption, such as certain regulated businesses

If you're in a regulated sector, check your specific position rather than assuming the general rule applies — healthcare and financial services businesses sometimes carry obligations that override the standard exemption.

## The Deadlines That Actually Matter

This is where directors most often come unstuck, because there isn't one deadline — there are several, and they don't line up. Working from your **accounting reference date** (ARD), which is normally the anniversary of the end of the month you incorporated in:

- **Companies House accounts** — due **9 months** after your ARD in a normal year

- **Your very first accounts** — due **21 months** after the date of incorporation, which is the one exception to the nine-month rule

- **Corporation Tax payment** — due **9 months and 1 day** after the end of your accounting period

- **Company Tax Return (CT600)** — due **12 months** after the end of your accounting period

Read that Corporation Tax line again, because it catches people out every year: you have to **pay before you file**. The money is due at nine months and a day, but the return explaining the calculation isn't due until twelve months. If you don't know the exact figure yet, pay your best estimate by the earlier date to avoid interest, then reconcile when the return goes in.

A worked example makes it concrete. For a company with a **31 March 2026** year end: Corporation Tax must be paid by **1 January 2027**, the accounts must reach Companies House by **31 December 2026**, and the CT600 is due to HMRC by **31 March 2027**.

Companies with profits above £1.5 million pay Corporation Tax in quarterly instalments instead, but that regime sits well outside the world most small companies live in.

## How It's Filed

Filing is electronic. A few practical notes:

- Accounts are filed in **iXBRL format**, which is both human-readable and machine-readable — your accounting software or accountant handles the tagging automatically

- Most companies file accounts through the Companies House **WebFiling** service using their authentication code

- Small companies and micro-entities can use the joint **Company Accounts and Tax Online (CATO)** service to file with Companies House and HMRC in one go

- The balance sheet must be approved by a director before submission

## What Happens If You're Late

Companies House penalties are automatic and escalate with time. For a private company, they currently charge from **£150** for accounts up to a month late, rising to **£1,500** for more than six months. Crucially, the penalty **doubles** if you file late in two consecutive financial years — so one slip makes the next one far more painful.

HMRC runs a separate penalty system for a late CT600: £100 immediately, another £100 at three months, and then tax-geared penalties of 10% of the unpaid tax at six months and a further 10% at twelve. File late for three years running and the flat £100 penalties jump to £500 each. And remember, late payment of the tax itself attracts interest from the day it was due, regardless of whether the return is in yet.

## A Few Habits Worth Building

- **Diarise every deadline** the day your accounting period starts — don't leave it to month eleven

- **Keep bookkeeping current** throughout the year so year-end is a tidy-up, not an excavation

- **Check your accounts and CT600 tell the same story** — mismatched figures invite questions from both Companies House and HMRC

- **Don't ignore an HMRC notice to file**, even for a dormant company, or penalties accrue regardless

- **Confirm your size category** before choosing a reporting standard — using FRS 105 when you should be on FRS 102 (or the reverse) is a common cause of rejection

Annual accounts reward a bit of order and punish last-minute scrambling, which is true of most things in running a company. The figures and deadlines above reflect the position for the 2025/26 tax year, but rules and thresholds do shift — and if your situation involves anything beyond a straightforward small company, a short conversation with an accountant tends to save far more than it costs.

*This article is general guidance for the 2025/26 UK tax year and reflects Companies House and HMRC rules at the time of writing. It is not a substitute for professional advice. For anything specific to your company, speak to a qualified accountant.*
