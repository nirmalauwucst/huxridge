# Corporation Tax: A Practical Guide for Company Directors

Corporation tax is the tax a limited company pays on its profits, and for many directors it's the single largest bill the business faces each year. Unlike the income tax that comes out of a salary, nobody deducts it for you as you go — the responsibility for calculating it correctly, paying it on time, and reporting it to HMRC sits squarely with the company. Get it right and it's simply a cost of doing business. Get it wrong and the interest and penalties stack up quietly in the background.

Whether you run a dental practice through a company, contract through a personal service company, or manage a growing healthcare business, the rules are the same. What follows is a plain-English walk through how corporation tax works in 2025/26 — the rates, what's actually taxed, the reliefs worth knowing about, and the deadlines you can't afford to miss.

## Who Pays It, and on What

Corporation tax applies to UK limited companies, as well as to some other organisations such as clubs and associations, and to overseas companies with a UK branch. It's charged on the company's **taxable profits**, which isn't quite the same as the profit figure you see at the bottom of your accounts. Taxable profit covers:

- **Trading profits** — the money the business makes from its main activities

- **Investment income** — interest and similar returns

- **Chargeable gains** — profits from selling assets such as property or equipment for more than they cost

The headline accounting profit is adjusted to get there: you add back costs that aren't allowable for tax, take off reliefs and allowances, and the result is the figure on which your tax is calculated. This is exactly why the number on your tax return rarely matches the profit in your management accounts.

## The Rates for 2025/26

Since April 2023, the UK has run a two-rate system, and the rates are unchanged for the 2025/26 financial year (covering accounting periods from 1 April 2025 to 31 March 2026):

- Small profits rate of **19%** — on taxable profits up to **£50,000**. Most UK companies fall here; HMRC figures suggest roughly 70% of active companies have profits below this line.

- Main rate of **25%** — on taxable profits above **£250,000**.

- **Marginal relief** — for profits between **£50,000 and £250,000**, a tapering mechanism bridges the two rates so there's no sudden cliff edge as profits grow.

There's a quirk in that middle band worth understanding. Although the main rate is 25%, the way marginal relief is withdrawn means each extra pound of profit between £50,000 and £250,000 is effectively taxed at around **26.5%** — higher than the headline rate above £250,000. In practice, this means a company sitting in the relief band pays more tax per pound than one comfortably over the top threshold, which is a genuine planning point rather than a rounding curiosity.

## Marginal Relief, Briefly

You don't need to memorise the formula — your accountant or filing software calculates it — but it helps to know the shape of it. The relief is worked out using a fraction of 3/200 applied to the gap between your profits and the £250,000 upper limit, which produces that gradual climb from an effective 19% up towards 25%. The effect is a smooth ramp rather than a step. If your profits are hovering around £50,000, even a modest increase tips you into the relief band, so it's an area where timing of income and expenditure can make a real difference to the bill.

## Associated Companies: A Trap Worth Knowing

Here's one that catches people running more than one company. The £50,000 and £250,000 thresholds are **divided between associated companies**. Broadly, companies are associated where one controls the other, or both are under common control.

The arithmetic is simple and unforgiving. If you control two associated companies, the thresholds halve — the small profits rate now only applies up to £25,000 each, and the main rate kicks in at £125,000 each. Three companies, and you're dividing by three. So a structure that looks tidy on paper can quietly push profits into higher effective rates far sooner than a single-company setup would. If you own or control multiple companies, this is worth modelling carefully before assuming the standard thresholds apply to each.

The same proportional division applies if your accounting period is shorter than 12 months — the thresholds are prorated accordingly.

## What You Can Deduct

You only pay tax on profit after allowable costs, so claiming everything you're entitled to directly reduces the bill. The general test is that a cost must be incurred **wholly and exclusively** for the trade. Common allowable expenses include:

- Staff salaries, employer's National Insurance, and pension contributions

- Rent, utilities, and business premises costs

- Travel, subscriptions, insurance, and professional fees

- Stock and raw materials

A few things are specifically *not* deductible — client entertaining is the classic example, and so is depreciation in your accounts (capital allowances take its place instead, see below). Drawing the line correctly matters because disallowable costs claimed in error are exactly what HMRC looks for.

## Capital Allowances on Equipment

When you buy assets the business will keep and use — equipment, computers, vans, fixtures — you can't simply deduct the cost as an expense. Instead, you claim **capital allowances**. The main routes for 2025/26 are:

- **Annual Investment Allowance (AIA)** — a 100% deduction on up to **£1 million** of qualifying plant and machinery each year, new or second-hand. This covers most of what a typical company buys.

- **Full expensing** — a permanent 100% first-year deduction for companies on qualifying new main-pool plant and machinery, with no upper limit. It's only available to companies (not sole traders or partnerships) and excludes assets bought to lease out.

- **Writing down allowances** — where full first-year relief doesn't apply, you deduct a percentage of the value each year: **18%** for the main pool and **6%** for the special rate pool, on a reducing-balance basis.

A practical note: cars don't qualify for AIA or full expensing, and relief depends on when the expenditure is incurred — usually the date you're unconditionally committed to pay, not the invoice date. For a company paying the 25% rate, every £100,000 of qualifying spend can trim up to £25,000 from that year's bill, so the timing of larger purchases is worth thinking about.

## Reliefs Worth Asking About

Beyond the everyday deductions, a few reliefs can materially cut the bill for the right company:

- **R&D tax relief** — if your company spends money solving genuine scientific or technological uncertainty, you may be able to claim enhanced relief. The rules have tightened in recent years, so claims need to be well-evidenced.

- **Trading losses** — a loss can usually be carried forward against future profits, or carried back against the previous year, generating a refund of tax already paid.

- **Group relief** — where companies sit in a group, losses in one can sometimes be set against profits in another.

These are worth a conversation with an accountant rather than a do-it-yourself attempt, because the eligibility conditions and the way relief is claimed can be intricate.

## The Deadlines — and the One That Catches Everyone

Corporation tax has an unusual rhythm: you have to **pay before you file**. Working from the end of your accounting period:

- **Payment** of corporation tax is due **9 months and 1 day** after the end of the accounting period

- Your **Company Tax Return (CT600)**, with accounts and computations, is due **12 months** after the end of the accounting period

That ordering trips up countless businesses. The money is due three months before the paperwork that explains it. If you don't know the precise figure by the payment date, pay your best estimate to stop interest accruing, then reconcile when you file. A worked example: for a company with a **31 March 2026** year end, corporation tax must be paid by **1 January 2027**, and the CT600 is due to HMRC by **31 March 2027**.

Companies with profits above **£1.5 million** follow a different regime and pay in quarterly instalments, with the very largest paying even earlier — but that sits well outside the world most small companies occupy.

## Filing, and What Happens If You're Late

The CT600 is filed online through your company's Corporation Tax account, linked to your Government Gateway credentials, and accounts are submitted in iXBRL format. Paper returns are effectively a thing of the past.

Penalties for a late return are automatic and escalate: **£100** the moment you miss the deadline, another £100 at three months, then tax-geared penalties of 10% of the unpaid tax at six months and a further 10% at twelve. File late for three years running and the flat £100 penalties rise to £500 each. Separately, **interest runs on unpaid tax** from the day it was due, whether or not the return is in, which is the real reason paying on time matters even when the return isn't ready.

One more point that catches dormant-company owners: if HMRC issues a notice to file, you must either submit the return or formally tell HMRC the company is dormant. Ignoring the notice triggers penalties regardless of whether any tax is owed.

## A Few Habits Worth Building

- **Set aside the tax as you earn it** — a separate account for corporation tax means the bill never comes as a cash-flow shock

- **Diarise both dates** — the 9-month payment and the 12-month filing — the day your accounting period starts

- **Keep clean records throughout the year** so the return is a tidy-up rather than an annual archaeology project

- **Count your associated companies** before assuming the full £50,000 and £250,000 thresholds apply to each one

- **Time significant purchases thoughtfully**, since capital allowances land in the period the spend is incurred

Corporation tax rewards a bit of planning and punishes the last-minute scramble. The rates, thresholds, and deadlines above reflect the position for the 2025/26 financial year, but tax rules shift with each Budget — and where your company's situation is anything beyond straightforward, a short conversation with an accountant usually pays for itself many times over.
